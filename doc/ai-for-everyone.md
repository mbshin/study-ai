## Week 1: Introduction

### Economic Value of AI

- McKinsey Global Institute estimates AI will create **$13–22 trillion** of additional value annually by 2033
- Generative AI accounts for **$3–4 trillion** of that total
- The rest comes from other AI forms like supervised learning

### Three Categories of AI

#### 1. Artificial Narrow Intelligence (ANI)

- Specialized systems that excel at a **single task**
- Examples: smart speakers, self-driving cars, web search, industrial automation
- "One-trick ponies" — but deliver tremendous value when applied well
- This is where most of today's real-world AI value comes from

#### 2. Generative AI

- Newer technology (ChatGPT, etc.) with **broader capabilities**
- Can handle multiple tasks: copyediting, brainstorming, text summarization, translation
- More versatile than traditional narrow AI but still has limitations

#### 3. Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)

- Theoretical AI matching or exceeding **human-level intelligence** across all domains
- Still very distant — requires multiple technological breakthroughs
- Potentially decades or centuries away
- Important to distinguish from ANI when evaluating AI claims

### Week 1 Learning Objectives

- Define AI and machine learning
- Understand data types and their value
- Identify characteristics of AI-first companies
- Recognize machine learning's capabilities **and limitations**
- (Optional) Deep learning and neural networks

### Key Takeaway: Limitations Matter

> "Newspapers as well as research papers tend to talk only about the success stories of machine learning."

- Media coverage skews perception of what AI can actually do
- Understanding **failure cases** is just as important as success stories
- Cutting through hype → realistic expectations about practical AI applications
